# AOTA Pre-Game Historical Framework (Border and Revanchism Support)

This companion document translates canon lore into implementation-facing world logic for upcoming border, claims, country setup, and diplomatic-content passes.

## Purpose
- Provide a concise historical spine from divergence to 1936.
- Map treaty logic to current geopolitical fault lines.
- Identify where grievance pressure should appear in focuses, events, ideas, and AI behavior.

## Historical spine in one view
1. **1917-1918: Central Powers non-defeat under exhaustion dynamics.**
2. **1919: Treaty of Antwerp creates a victor-shaped but incomplete peace.**
3. **1919-1926: Follow-up protocol era (Danube, Rhine, Levant, borderland commissions, colonial compensation).**
4. **Late 1920s: Debt and security crises militarize politics.**
5. **Early 1930s: Conferences lose legitimacy; frontier incidents become strategic signaling.**
6. **1936: Armed intermission system reaches breaking point.**

## Treaty-to-border mapping guide

### Armistice of The Hague (1918)
- Function: freeze fronts, stop attritional collapse.
- Border effect: creates temporary control lines later formalized or contested.

### Treaty of Antwerp (1919)
- Function: foundational peace architecture.
- Border effect:
  - German retention gains in key western/eastern sectors.
  - Danubian restoration under Habsburg authority.
  - Ottoman legal continuity in Levant/Mesopotamia.
  - Multiple unresolved access/corridor clauses deferred.

### Vienna Danubian Instruments (1919-1921)
- Function: keep imperial Danube together.
- Border effect: internal imperial borders preserved as administrative lines, not nation-state final borders.

### Rhine Statute of Luxembourg (1921)
- Function: contain immediate Franco-German war risk.
- Border effect: security zones, inspections, and legal revision channels without solving sovereignty narratives.

### Baltic-Black Sea Commission Accords (Riga, 1922)
- Function: manage post-Russian collapse frontier ambiguity.
- Border effect: interim entities, disputed legal inheritances, and militarized border commissions.

### Warsaw Guarantees Protocol (Antwerp Annex, 1919)
- Function: preserve a constrained Polish state as a buffer/transit compromise.
- Border effect: Poland remains independent but strategically boxed-in, producing corridor grievance and permanent security anxiety.

### Kyiv Provisional Sovereignty Act (Riga Annex, 1922)
- Function: legalize a Ukrainian republic after failed Russian reconsolidation.
- Border effect: UKR exists as an independent state in law but under continual external pressure from both revisionist and reunification projects.

### Straits and Levant Protocols of Smyrna (1920)
- Function: preserve Ottoman sovereignty while opening constrained international oversight.
- Border effect: Ottoman continuity with embedded insurgency triggers.

### Cairo Debt & Canal Convention (1924)
- Function: secure imperial debt/canal routes.
- Border effect: stronger external control over Egyptian strategic infrastructure.

### Colonial Compensation Protocols of Brussels (1926)
- Function: settle inter-imperial colonial claims through compensation and concession swaps.
- Border effect: legalizes unpopular colonial continuities and fuels anti-colonial radicalization.

## Regional grievance matrix (design-facing)

### Central Europe / Danube
- Primary grievances: blocked national consolidation, imperial overrule, minority securitization.
- Escalation triggers: succession crises, mobilization law, external patron intervention.

### Balkans
- Primary grievances: imposed balances, denied sovereignty projects, border asymmetry.
- Escalation triggers: paramilitary incidents, minority rights crises, Danubian collapse scares.

### Eastern Europe / Russian borderlands
- Primary grievances: interim borders becoming permanent without consent.
- Escalation triggers: reunification campaigns, anti-bolshevik/anti-imperial uprisings, corridor shutdowns.

### Ottoman / Middle East
- Primary grievances: imperial legal continuity without provincial legitimacy.
- Escalation triggers: tax revolts, rail/oil corridor sabotage, foreign-backed autonomy fronts.

### Colonial / extra-European
- Primary grievances: wartime sacrifice without postwar sovereignty gains.
- Escalation triggers: labor insurrection, concession violence, debt-enforcement interventions.

## What this framework should drive next
- Border implementation should reference the specific settlement instrument that created each line.
- Claims/revanchism content should pair emotional memory with legal-historical argument.
- Country setup should distinguish between:
  - fully sovereign states,
  - externally guaranteed states,
  - constrained/autonomous administrations,
  - stateless-but-organized national projects.

## Remaining ambiguities for future canon clarification

2. Which frontier entities are intended as recognized sovereigns at game start versus latent claims.
3. Degree of formal versus informal Ottoman autonomy granted in specific Arab provinces.
4. Whether colonial possession continuity is intended to be fully canonical or partly scenario-variable by content path.
5. Final canonical handling of high-friction ownership anomalies flagged in ownership audits (especially where content-complete tags start stateless).


## 1936 state-presence clarifications (for border implementation)
- **YUG/HUN:** treat as non-sovereign-at-start political projects in the Danubian space unless a later scripted rupture creates territorial sovereignty.
- **UKR:** must be explicitly referenced in eastern settlement logic as an independent-but-contested state (not a GER or RUS puppet at start), with independence grounded in the Kyiv Provisional Sovereignty Act.
- **POL:** persists as a constrained treaty state because the Warsaw Guarantees Protocol preserved it as a buffer compromise instead of an ethnographically settled final peace.
- **SER:** model as a client-security state under Austro-Danubian strategic primacy (local administration, external constraint).
